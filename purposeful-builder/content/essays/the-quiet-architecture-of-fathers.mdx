There is a distinct kind of love that men carry. It is rarely loud. It is rarely expressive in the way the world expects love to be. Instead, a father’s love is architectural. It is built into the foundations of a home. It is the roof that takes the beating of the storm so that those inside can sleep peacefully.

Today is Father’s Day. 

I’ve been thinking about the silent weight that fathers carry. Society often measures men by what they provide, but rarely acknowledges what it costs them to do so. We see the provision, but we rarely see the exhaustion. We see the structure, but we rarely see the sleepless nights spent worrying if that structure will hold.

To be a father is to accept that you will often be misunderstood. Your discipline will sometimes be seen as harshness. Your long hours will sometimes be seen as absence. Your quietness will sometimes be seen as distance. But underneath all of that is a deep, overwhelming desire to protect, to build, and to leave something behind that outlasts you.

We are quick to celebrate the emotional labor of mothers—and rightly so. But we must also learn to see and celebrate the structural labor of fathers.

To the men who wake up every day and put their shoulders to the wheel, even when their strength is failing: we see you.

To the fathers who are breaking generational cycles, choosing to be present in ways their own fathers were not: we honor you.

To the men who stand in the gap as fathers to boys who have none—mentoring, guiding, and showing them what safe, disciplined masculinity looks like: your work is eternal.

Happy Father’s Day to the builders. May your foundations hold, and may you know that your silent sacrifices are never truly unseen.
